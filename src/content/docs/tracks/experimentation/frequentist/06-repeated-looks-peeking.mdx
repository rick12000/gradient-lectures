Continuously monitoring a dashboard and stopping an experiment the moment $p < 0.05$ is a **temporal form of [multiple testing](/gradient-lectures/tracks/experimentation/frequentist/05-multiple-testing/)**. You are effectively running a new hypothesis test at every data increment on the same accumulating stream.

Crucially, **just looking at the data is not the problem**. The statistical error only occurs if you *act* — i.e., stop the test early and declare a winner. If you observe the dashboard but wait for the predetermined sample size to finish, your false positive rate remains at the nominal 5%.

Unlike independent multiple comparisons (which Bonferroni can correct), the sequence of test statistics $Z_n$ over time is a highly **autocorrelated** process. Day 6's data contains all of Day 5's data plus a small increment. This means there is no closed-form formula for the FPR inflation — it must be estimated empirically.

## Peeking Error Inflation

To see why autocorrelation is dangerous, consider 50 A/B tests simulated under $H_0$ (no true effect), tracking the test statistic $Z_n$ from $n=1$ to $n=1000$ users per group.

Despite no true effect, the paths wander widely and frequently cross the $p < 0.05$ boundary. Each path breaches the boundary at some point because variance accumulates over time, allowing the random walk of the test statistic to eventually hit the threshold given enough chances.

In practice, calculating significance at any point over $N=1000$ yields a dramatically inflated false positive rate — often near 50% — compared to the expected 5%. By contrast, evaluating only at the final sample size keeps the FPR close to the nominal level.

## Fixed Horizon Testing

The simplest behavioral fix: **strict adherence to a fixed sample size**. Hide p-values and confidence intervals until the predetermined $N$ is reached. Commit to not stopping early regardless of what the dashboard shows.

The only legitimate exception is a "Do No Harm" guardrail (e.g., a revenue crash or surge in error rates). In those cases, the goal shifts from hypothesis confirmation to risk mitigation, and the statistical penalty is accepted.

## Group Sequential Testing

If we must peek and retain the option to stop early, but can restrict ourselves to a small number of **planned** interim looks (e.g., weekly check-ins), we use **Group Sequential Testing**.

We distribute our $\alpha$ budget across $K$ planned looks via an **$\alpha$-spending function**. Instead of a constant $Z = 1.96$ boundary, we require overwhelming evidence early on and relax the threshold as more data accumulates.

### O'Brien-Fleming Boundary

The **O'Brien-Fleming (OBF)** boundary scales the threshold inversely with the square root of the information fraction $t_k = n_k / N$:


$$
Z_k = \frac{C_{\text{OBF}}}{\sqrt{t_k}}
$$



For $K = 5$ looks at overall $\alpha = 0.05$, the constant $C_{\text{OBF}} \approx 2.040$. The boundary starts very wide at the first look and narrows to approximately 1.96 at the final look.

<Callout type="note" title="How OBF controls FWER" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

Requiring a much larger test statistic to stop early prevents most spurious crossings. Crucially, we **only** evaluate the test statistic at the $K$ planned intervals. A path that wanders above the theoretical boundary curve between planned look times is irrelevant — we aren't looking.

By requiring a larger $Z_k$ early, the OBF boundary successfully distributes the $\alpha$ budget so that the total probability of a false crossing across all $K$ looks remains $\leq \alpha$.

</Callout>

Group Sequential Testing is well-suited to slow-moving experiments with naturally batched data (e.g., clinical trials). It is impractical for continuously updating digital dashboards where $K$ is effectively infinite.

## Always-Valid Inference

For real-time dashboards, we need a boundary valid at *every* point in time. This requires abandoning fixed-horizon inference and moving to **Always-Valid p-values** and **Confidence Sequences**, built on the **Mixture Sequential Probability Ratio Test (mSPRT)**.

### The mSPRT Likelihood Ratio

The mSPRT computes a likelihood ratio $\Lambda_n$ by integrating over a mixture distribution $F(\theta)$ of possible effect sizes:


$$
\Lambda_n = \int \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{f_\theta(X_i)}{f_0(X_i)}\, dF(\theta)
$$



This ratio compares the likelihood of the data under a range of alternatives versus the null.

### The Martingale Property

Under $H_0$, $\Lambda_n$ is a **non-negative martingale** — its expected future value, given all past information, equals its current value:


$$
\mathbb{E}[\Lambda_{n+1} \mid \Lambda_n] = \Lambda_n
$$



Since $\Lambda_0 = 1$, the expected value at any time is 1.

### Ville's Inequality

The martingale property allows us to apply **Ville's Inequality**:


$$
P\!\left(\exists\, n \geq 1: \Lambda_n \geq \frac{1}{\alpha}\right) \leq \alpha
$$



This guarantees that if $H_0$ is true, the probability that $\Lambda_n$ ever crosses $1/\alpha$ at **any sample size** is bounded by $\alpha$.

Whenever $\Lambda_n \geq 1/\alpha$, we can stop and reject $H_0$ with guaranteed FPR $\leq \alpha$.

### Confidence Sequences

By mathematically inverting this test, we construct a **Confidence Sequence** — a sequence of intervals that simultaneously covers the true effect at *all* time steps $n$:


$$
P\!\left(\forall\, n \geq 1: \Delta \in \text{CS}_n\right) \geq 1 - \alpha
$$



Unlike a standard confidence interval (valid only at a fixed $N$), confidence sequences remain valid regardless of when we stop. This is the mathematical foundation for modern always-valid experiment monitoring at tech platforms.
