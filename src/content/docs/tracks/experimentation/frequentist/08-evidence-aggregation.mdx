Frustrated that you ran the same A/B test three times and it came *just short* of significance every time?

Under the null hypothesis, p-values are uniformly distributed on $(0, 1)$. A run like $\{0.07, 0.09, 0.11\}$ is cumulatively improbable — if nothing is happening, seeing three consecutive near-misses is unlikely. Evidence aggregation methods formalize this intuition: they combine $k$ independent p-values into a single global test statistic with a known null distribution.

Two classical procedures do this via different transformations: **Fisher's method** (logarithmic) and **Stouffer's method** (normal quantile).

## Fisher's Method

Under the null hypothesis, each p-value is uniformly distributed: $p_i \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$.

Fisher's method transforms each p-value into a chi-squared variable, then exploits the additivity of independent chi-squared distributions.

<Callout type="note" title="Derivation: Fisher's combined probability test" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

**Step 1 — Transform the uniform p-value**

Apply $-\ln(p_i)$. To find its distribution, compute the CDF:


$$
P(-\ln(p_i) \leq x) = P(p_i \geq e^{-x}) = 1 - e^{-x}
$$



This is the CDF of an **Exponential(1)** distribution.

**Step 2 — Scale to chi-squared**

Multiply by 2: $-2\ln(p_i)$. An Exponential(rate $1/2$) distribution is identical to a $\chi^2_2$ distribution:


$$
-2\ln(p_i) \sim \chi^2_2
$$



**Step 3 — Sum across independent experiments**

Independent chi-squared variables sum to another chi-squared with degrees of freedom equal to the sum:


$$
\chi^2_{\text{global}} = \sum_{i=1}^k -2\ln(p_i) \sim \chi^2_{2k}
$$



</Callout>

**Procedure:**
1. Compute $X^2 = -2\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^k \ln(p_i)$
2. Evaluate the global p-value as $P(\chi^2_{2k} \geq X^2)$

<Callout type="example" title="Fisher's method worked example" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

Three experiments: $p_1 = 0.07$, $p_2 = 0.09$, $p_3 = 0.11$ — all above $\alpha = 0.05$.

**Transform:**
- $-2\ln(0.07) \approx 5.319$
- $-2\ln(0.09) \approx 4.816$
- $-2\ln(0.11) \approx 4.415$

**Sum:** $X^2 = 5.319 + 4.816 + 4.415 = 14.549$

**Evaluate against $\chi^2_6$:** $p_{\text{global}} = P(\chi^2_6 \geq 14.549) \approx 0.024$

Significant at $\alpha = 0.05$. Under the null, observing three p-values this consistently small would happen only 2.4% of the time.

</Callout>

**Key property of Fisher's method:** Because $-\ln(p) \to +\infty$ as $p \to 0$, a single extreme p-value can **dominate the sum** and virtually guarantee significance regardless of the other experiments. This is a strength when screening for *any* signal, and a liability when you need *consistent* evidence across all experiments.

## Stouffer's Z-Score Method

Stouffer's method transforms each p-value into a standard normal variable, then exploits the linearity of normal distributions.

<Callout type="note" title="Derivation: Stouffer's Z-score method" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

**Step 1 — Map to standard normal**

Apply the inverse normal CDF $\Phi^{-1}$ to the complement, ensuring small p-values map to large positive Z-scores:


$$
Z_i = \Phi^{-1}(1 - p_i) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1) \quad \text{under } H_0
$$



**Step 2 — Sum the Z-scores**


$$
S = \sum_{i=1}^k Z_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, k)
$$



**Step 3 — Standardize**

Divide by $\sqrt{k}$ to restore variance 1:


$$
Z_{\text{global}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^k Z_i}{\sqrt{k}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)
$$



</Callout>

**Procedure:**
1. Compute $Z_i = \Phi^{-1}(1 - p_i)$ for each test
2. Compute $Z = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^k Z_i}{\sqrt{k}}$
3. Evaluate the global p-value as $P(\mathcal{N}(0,1) \geq Z)$

<Callout type="example" title="Stouffer's method worked example" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

Same three experiments: $p_1 = 0.07$, $p_2 = 0.09$, $p_3 = 0.11$.

**Map to Z-scores:**
- $Z_1 = \Phi^{-1}(0.93) \approx 1.476$
- $Z_2 = \Phi^{-1}(0.91) \approx 1.341$
- $Z_3 = \Phi^{-1}(0.89) \approx 1.227$

**Sum and standardize:** $Z = (1.476 + 1.341 + 1.227) / \sqrt{3} \approx 2.334$

**Global p-value:** $P(\mathcal{N}(0,1) \geq 2.334) \approx 0.010$

Significant. Unlike Fisher's method, this result was driven by all three experiments leaning consistently in the same direction — no single outlier could have produced this Z-score.

</Callout>

**Key property of Stouffer's method:** The Z-score mapping grows slowly as $p \to 0$. No single extreme p-value can dominate to anything like the same degree as in Fisher's method. Stouffer rewards **directional consistency**.

## Comparison: Fisher vs. Stouffer

| | Fisher | Stouffer |
|---|---|---|
| Transform | $-2\ln(p_i)$ | $\Phi^{-1}(1-p_i)$ |
| Null distribution | $\chi^2_{2k}$ | $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ |
| Sensitivity to outliers | **High** — one tiny p dominates | **Low** — no single outlier dominates |
| Rewards | Any extreme signal | Consistent directional evidence |
| Supports weighting | Not in closed form | Yes (see below) |

## Weighted Stouffer's Method

When $k$ experiments differ substantially in sample size, treating them as equal contributors distorts the aggregate. A larger experiment is more reliable — its p-value is more stable and should carry more weight.

**Why weight by $\sqrt{N_i}$:**

A Z-score from $N_i$ observations has precision proportional to $\sqrt{N_i}$. Assigning $w_i = \sqrt{N_i}$ makes each experiment's contribution proportional to its precision — the generalized least-squares solution.

**The weighted statistic:**


$$
Z_w = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^k w_i Z_i}{\sqrt{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^k w_i^2}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)
$$



<Callout type="note" title="Why this doesn't bias the result" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

Under $H_0$, each $Z_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$, so $\mathbb{E}[T] = \sum_i w_i \cdot 0 = 0$. The weights cannot shift the null distribution. The variance of $T = \sum w_i Z_i$ is $\sum w_i^2$ (by independence). Dividing by $\sqrt{\sum w_i^2}$ standardizes to variance 1. The null distribution $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ is preserved regardless of the weights — no recalibration of critical values is needed.

</Callout>

<Callout type="warning" title="The independence assumption" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

Both Fisher and Stouffer assume the $k$ experiments are **independent**. If two experiments share subjects or overlap in time, their Z-scores will be positively correlated. $\text{Var}(T)$ will exceed $\sum w_i^2$, the denominator will underestimate the true spread, and the test will be too liberal (inflated false positive rate).

In such cases, a correlation correction (Strube's extension or full GLS) must be applied.

</Callout>
