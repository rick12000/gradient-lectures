We've seen how [Bayes Rule](/gradient-lectures/tracks/bayesian-statistics/01-bayes-rule/) allows us to update the probability of a discrete event. Now, instead of calculating a single posterior probability, we calculate a **posterior probability distribution**.

## The Problem

Consider a coin flip. The coin can land heads or tails. We want to estimate $\theta$, the true probability that the coin lands on heads.

A **frequentist** approach: flip the coin $n$ times, observe $h$ heads, and state $\theta = h/n$. If we flip 10 times and get 5 heads, the probability is 50%.

A **Bayesian** approach allows us to factor in extra considerations before we even flip the coin. Is the coin fair? Has it been tampered with? We can bake this prior information into our probability estimations.

## The Bayesian Framework

We start with the standard Bayes formula:


$$
P(\theta|D) = \frac{P(D|\theta)P(\theta)}{P(D)}
$$



Each component plays a specific role.

### The Likelihood: $P(D|\theta)$

The likelihood is the probability of observing the data $D$ if the parameter were exactly $\theta$. Since a coin flip is a binary event, this is a Bernoulli distribution. If we observe $h$ heads and $t$ tails:


$$
P(D|\theta) = \theta^h (1-\theta)^t
$$



### The Scaling Constant: $P(D)$

The denominator $P(D)$ is the total probability of observing the data across all possible values of $\theta$, ensuring the posterior integrates to 1:


$$
P(D) = \int_0^1 P(D|\theta)P(\theta)\, d\theta
$$



Calculating this integral is often complex. We have no control over the likelihood part, but we do have control over the prior — and we can choose a prior that makes this integral tractable.

### The Prior: $P(\theta)$

The prior $P(\theta)$ represents our subjective assumptions about $\theta$ before seeing any data. We want a prior that **merges well with the Bernoulli likelihood** and results in a closed-form posterior.

The **Beta distribution** is defined on $[0, 1]$, matching the domain of our Bernoulli likelihood. It has two shape parameters, $\alpha$ and $\beta$:


$$
P(\theta) = \text{Beta}(\theta|\alpha, \beta) = \frac{1}{B(\alpha, \beta)} \theta^{\alpha-1} (1-\theta)^{\beta-1}
$$



where $B(\alpha, \beta)$ is the Beta function (a normalizing constant).

<Callout type="tip" title="Interpreting the Beta parameters">

The parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$ can be interpreted as **pseudo-counts** from imaginary prior observations:
- $\alpha - 1$ represents prior "successes" (heads)
- $\beta - 1$ represents prior "failures" (tails)

A Beta$(1, 1)$ prior is perfectly flat — every value of $\theta$ from 0 to 1 is equally plausible. This is the **uniform prior**, expressing complete ignorance. A Beta$(2, 2)$ prior places more mass near $\theta = 0.5$, encoding a mild belief in fairness.

</Callout>

### The Posterior: $P(\theta|D)$

<Callout type="note" title="Derivation: Beta-Bernoulli Posterior" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

**Step 1 — Write Bayes' Rule in Full**


$$
P(\theta \mid D) = \frac{P(D \mid \theta)P(\theta)}{\int_0^1 P(D \mid \theta)P(\theta)\,d\theta}
$$



**Step 2 — Write the Prior**


$$
P(\theta) = \frac{1}{B(\alpha,\beta)} \theta^{\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{\beta-1}
$$



**Step 3 — Write the Likelihood**

For $h$ heads and $t$ tails:


$$
P(D \mid \theta) = \theta^h(1-\theta)^t
$$



**Step 4 — Form the Numerator**


$$
P(D \mid \theta)P(\theta) = \frac{1}{B(\alpha,\beta)} \theta^{h+\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{t+\beta-1}
$$



This has exactly the form of a Beta distribution — but with updated parameters $\alpha + h$ and $\beta + t$!

**Step 5 — Recognize the Posterior Family**

Since the numerator has the kernel of a Beta$(\alpha + h, \beta + t)$ distribution, the posterior must be:


$$
P(\theta \mid D) = \text{Beta}(\alpha + h,\; \beta + t)
$$



The denominator $P(D) = B(\alpha+h, \beta+t) / B(\alpha, \beta)$ is just the normalizing constant that makes this a valid density — it doesn't need to be computed separately because we've recognized the family.

</Callout>

The posterior is:


$$
\boxed{P(\theta \mid D) = \text{Beta}(\alpha + h,\; \beta + t)}
$$



This is the **conjugate update rule**: the Beta prior is a conjugate prior for the Bernoulli likelihood, meaning the posterior is again a Beta distribution. The update is trivially simple — just add the observed counts to the prior pseudo-counts.

## Worked Example

Suppose we start with a weakly informative prior Beta$(2, 2)$, encoding a mild belief that the coin is fair. We then observe 10 flips: 7 heads and 3 tails.

The posterior is:


$$
P(\theta|D) = \text{Beta}(2 + 7,\; 2 + 3) = \text{Beta}(9, 5)
$$



The posterior is a compromise between the prior (centered at 0.5) and the likelihood of the observed data (centered at 0.7).

## Results

From the posterior Beta$(9, 5)$ we can extract:

- **Point estimate:** The posterior mean is $\frac{\alpha_n}{\alpha_n + \beta_n} = \frac{9}{14} \approx 0.643$
- **Credible interval:** Grab the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of Beta$(9, 5)$ directly

Notice the posterior mean of $0.643$ is pulled between the prior mean ($0.5$) and the data mean ($0.7$) — this is **Bayesian shrinkage**.
