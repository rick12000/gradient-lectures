description: Bayesian estimation of a full coefficient vector via matrix-valued Normal-Normal conjugacy. Connects to ridge regression and OLS as limiting cases.
---

import Callout from '../../../../components/Callout.astro';

This note extends [Bayesian Linear Regression](/gradient-lectures/tracks/bayesian-statistics/04-bayesian-linear-regression/) from a single coefficient to a full coefficient vector. It uses the same [Normal-Normal conjugacy](/gradient-lectures/tracks/bayesian-statistics/03-normal-normal/), but writes the update in **matrix form** so that all $p$ coefficients are estimated simultaneously.

The main conceptual upgrade: instead of one number, we estimate uncertainty about several coefficients at once, and those uncertainties can interact.

---

## 1. The Setup

Suppose we observe $n$ data points, each with $p$ predictors. Stack the observations:


$$
\mathbf{y} = X\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2 I_n)
$$



where $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ is the design matrix with rows $\mathbf{x}_i^T$, and the coefficient vector is $\boldsymbol{\beta} \in \mathbb{R}^p$.

The likelihood in matrix form:


$$
\mathbf{y} \mid X, \boldsymbol{\beta} \sim \mathcal{N}(X\boldsymbol{\beta},\; \sigma^2 I_n)
$$



<Callout type="tip" title="Including an intercept" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

To include an intercept, add a column of ones to $X$:


$$
\mathbf{x}_i^T = \begin{bmatrix}1 & x_{i1} & x_{i2} & \cdots\end{bmatrix}
$$



The first coefficient in $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ then becomes the intercept.

</Callout>

---

## 2. The Prior


$$
\boldsymbol{\beta} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{m}_0,\; S_0)
$$



where $\mathbf{m}_0 \in \mathbb{R}^p$ is the prior mean vector and $S_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times p}$ is the prior covariance matrix ($S_0^{-1}$ is the prior precision matrix).

A common simple choice is an independent isotropic prior:


$$
S_0 = \tau_0^2\, I_p
$$



This says we treat each coefficient as independent and equally uncertain before seeing any data.

<Callout type="note" title="What covariance means here" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

In the single-coefficient case, posterior uncertainty was a single variance $\tau_n^2$. In the multivariate case, it is a full covariance matrix. The diagonal entries measure uncertainty about each coefficient individually. The off-diagonal entries capture whether uncertainty in one coefficient moves together with uncertainty in another.

This matters when two predictors are correlated: the data might tell us a lot about their combined effect but little about which one deserves individual credit. The posterior covariance reflects this with wide uncertainty in some directions and narrow uncertainty in others.

</Callout>

---

## 3. The Likelihood in Matrix Form


$$
P(\mathbf{y} \mid X, \boldsymbol{\beta}) \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(\mathbf{y}-X\boldsymbol{\beta})^T(\mathbf{y}-X\boldsymbol{\beta})\right)
$$



The quadratic $(\mathbf{y}-X\boldsymbol{\beta})^T(\mathbf{y}-X\boldsymbol{\beta})$ is the matrix analog of $\sum_i(y_i - \beta x_i)^2$.

---

## 4. The Posterior

Because the likelihood is Normal in $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ and the prior is multivariate Normal, the posterior is also multivariate Normal:


$$
\boldsymbol{\beta} \mid \mathbf{y}, X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{m}_n,\; S_n)
$$



**Posterior precision** (matrix precision addition):


$$
\boxed{S_n^{-1} = S_0^{-1} + \frac{1}{\sigma^2}X^TX}
$$



**Posterior covariance:**


$$
S_n = \left(S_0^{-1} + \frac{1}{\sigma^2}X^TX\right)^{-1}
$$



**Posterior mean:**


$$
\boxed{\mathbf{m}_n = S_n\!\left(S_0^{-1}\mathbf{m}_0 + \frac{1}{\sigma^2}X^T\mathbf{y}\right)}
$$



The scalar-to-matrix correspondence is direct:

| Scalar case | Multivariate case |
|---|---|
| $\tau_0^2$ | $S_0$ |
| $\mu_0$ | $\mathbf{m}_0$ |
| $\sum x_i^2$ | $X^TX$ |
| $\sum x_i y_i$ | $X^T\mathbf{y}$ |
| $\tau_n^2$ | $S_n$ |
| $\mu_n$ | $\mathbf{m}_n$ |

---

## 5. Connection to OLS and Ridge Regression

<Callout type="tip" title="Weak prior â†’ OLS" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

As the prior becomes uninformative ($S_0^{-1} \to 0$), the posterior mean approaches the OLS estimate (assuming $X^TX$ is invertible):


$$
\mathbf{m}_n \to (X^TX)^{-1}X^T\mathbf{y} = \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{\text{OLS}}
$$



and the posterior covariance approaches the standard OLS sampling covariance:


$$
S_n \to \sigma^2(X^TX)^{-1}
$$



OLS is the **weak-prior limiting case** of Bayesian linear regression.

</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="Zero-mean isotropic prior â†’ Ridge regression" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

Set $\mathbf{m}_0 = \mathbf{0}$ and $S_0 = \tau_0^2 I_p$. Then:


$$
\mathbf{m}_n = \left(X^TX + \frac{\sigma^2}{\tau_0^2}I_p\right)^{-1}X^T\mathbf{y}
$$



This has exactly the form of **ridge regression** with penalty $\lambda = \sigma^2/\tau_0^2$.

The Bayesian interpretation: ridge regression corresponds to a zero-centered Normal prior that shrinks coefficients toward zero. The regularization strength is the ratio of noise variance to prior variance.

</Callout>

---

## 6. Making Predictions

For a new predictor vector $\mathbf{x}_*$, we average over the posterior:

**Predictive mean:**


$$
\mathbb{E}[y_* \mid \mathbf{x}_*, \mathbf{y}, X] = \mathbf{x}_*^T\mathbf{m}_n
$$



**Predictive variance** (coefficient uncertainty + observation noise):


$$
\text{Var}(y_* \mid \mathbf{x}_*, \mathbf{y}, X) = \mathbf{x}_*^T S_n \mathbf{x}_* + \sigma^2
$$



**Posterior predictive distribution:**


$$
\boxed{y_* \mid \mathbf{x}_*,\mathbf{y},X \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mathbf{x}_*^T\mathbf{m}_n,\;\; \mathbf{x}_*^T S_n \mathbf{x}_* + \sigma^2\right)}
$$



<Callout type="note" title="Credible interval for the mean response only" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

For uncertainty about the mean $\mathbf{x}_*^T\boldsymbol{\beta}$ only (excluding future noise):


$$
\mathbf{x}_*^T\mathbf{m}_n \;\pm\; 1.96\sqrt{\mathbf{x}_*^T S_n \mathbf{x}_*}
$$



</Callout>

### Why Prediction Uncertainty is Directional

In the scalar case, uncertainty grew with $x_*^2 \tau_n^2$. In the multivariate case, the analog is:


$$
\mathbf{x}_*^T S_n \mathbf{x}_*
$$



This quantity depends on **direction**, not just magnitude. If $\mathbf{x}_*$ points in a direction that was well-constrained by the training data, $\mathbf{x}_*^T S_n \mathbf{x}_*$ will be small. If it points in a poorly observed direction, uncertainty can be much larger.

The regression line from the scalar case has become a regression plane (or hyperplane). The posterior wiggles the whole plane with different amounts of flexibility in different directions.

## 7. Worked Example

Two predictors, no intercept:


$$
X = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1\end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix}1.0 \\ 2.0 \\ 2.8\end{bmatrix}
$$



Known noise $\sigma^2 = 1$, weak zero-centered prior $\boldsymbol{\beta} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\; 4I)$, so $S_0^{-1} = \frac{1}{4}I$.

**Data summaries:**


$$
X^TX = \begin{bmatrix}2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2\end{bmatrix}, \qquad X^T\mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix}3.8 \\ 4.8\end{bmatrix}
$$



**Posterior precision:**


$$
S_n^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix}0.25 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.25\end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2.25 & 1 \\ 1 & 2.25\end{bmatrix}
$$



**Posterior covariance** (invert $S_n^{-1}$):


$$
S_n \approx \begin{bmatrix}0.554 & -0.246 \\ -0.246 & 0.554\end{bmatrix}
$$



**Posterior mean:**


$$
\mathbf{m}_n = S_n X^T\mathbf{y} \approx \begin{bmatrix}0.923 \\ 1.723\end{bmatrix}
$$



So $\beta_1 \approx 0.923$ and $\beta_2 \approx 1.723$. Notice the **negative off-diagonal** in $S_n$: if $\beta_1$ is higher than expected, $\beta_2$ tends to be lower. This reflects the data partially trading off the two coefficients against each other.

**Prediction at $\mathbf{x}_* = [1, 1]^T$:**


$$
\text{Predictive mean} = 0.923 + 1.723 = 2.646
$$




$$
\text{Coefficient uncertainty} = \mathbf{x}_*^T S_n \mathbf{x}_* \approx 0.615
$$




$$
y_* \mid \mathbf{x}_* \sim \mathcal{N}(2.646,\; 1.615) \qquad [= 0.615 + 1.000]
$$

## 8. Summary

| | Scalar case | Multivariate case |
|---|---|---|
| Prior | $\beta \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \tau_0^2)$ | $\boldsymbol{\beta} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{m}_0, S_0)$ |
| Posterior precision | $\tau_n^{-2} = \tau_0^{-2} + \sigma^{-2}\sum x_i^2$ | $S_n^{-1} = S_0^{-1} + \sigma^{-2}X^TX$ |
| Posterior mean | $\mu_n = \tau_n^2(\tau_0^{-2}\mu_0 + \sigma^{-2}\sum x_iy_i)$ | $\mathbf{m}_n = S_n(S_0^{-1}\mathbf{m}_0 + \sigma^{-2}X^T\mathbf{y})$ |
| Predictive variance | $x_*^2\tau_n^2 + \sigma^2$ | $\mathbf{x}_*^T S_n \mathbf{x}_* + \sigma^2$ |

<Callout type="tip" title="What if ÏƒÂ² is unknown?" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

This note assumes known observation variance $\sigma^2$, matching the simplifying assumption in [Bayesian Linear Regression](/gradient-lectures/tracks/bayesian-statistics/04-bayesian-linear-regression/) and the [Normal-Normal model](/gradient-lectures/tracks/bayesian-statistics/03-normal-normal/).

If $\sigma^2$ is unknown, we need a prior over it too. A common conjugate choice is a **Normal-Inverse-Gamma** prior, which gives a different posterior and a Student-$t$ posterior predictive distribution.

</Callout>
