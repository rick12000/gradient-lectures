This note derives **Bayesian linear regression** in its simplest form: a linear regression with **one coefficient** $\beta$ and no intercept:


$$
y_i = \beta x_i + \epsilon_i
$$



The derivation is a direct application of the [Normal-Normal conjugate update](/gradient-lectures/tracks/bayesian-statistics/03-normal-normal/).

---

## 1. The Setup

Suppose we observe data $D = \{(x_1,y_1), \dots, (x_n,y_n)\}$. We assume the data are generated by a linear process with Gaussian noise $\epsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$:


$$
y_i = \beta x_i + \epsilon_i
$$



<Callout type="tip" title="What changes with an intercept?" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

With an intercept, the model becomes $y_i = \alpha + \beta x_i + \epsilon_i$. We would then need a joint prior over both $\alpha$ and $\beta$. The same conjugate logic applies, but the posterior becomes a **multivariate Gaussian** instead of a one-dimensional Gaussian. See [Multivariate Bayesian Linear Regression](/gradient-lectures/tracks/bayesian-statistics/05-multivariate-bayesian-lr/).

</Callout>

---

## 2. The Prior

Before seeing the data, we place a Normal prior over $\beta$:


$$
\beta \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0,\, \tau_0^2)
$$



Here $\mu_0$ is the prior mean (where we expect $\beta$ to be) and $\tau_0^2$ is the prior variance (how strongly we believe that expectation).

---

## 3. The Likelihood

Assuming observations are conditionally independent given $\beta$:


$$
P(D \mid \beta) = \prod_{i=1}^n P(y_i \mid x_i,\beta) = (2\pi\sigma^2)^{-n/2} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i-\beta x_i)^2\right)
$$



---

## 4. The Posterior

Because the likelihood is Gaussian in $\beta$ and the prior is Gaussian, this is a **Normal-Normal conjugate update**. The algebra is identical to the [Normal-Normal derivation](/gradient-lectures/tracks/bayesian-statistics/03-normal-normal/) with the substitution $y_i \to y_i / x_i$ being implicit in the sufficient statistics.

The posterior is:


$$
\beta \mid D \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_n,\; \tau_n^2)
$$



**Posterior precision** (precisions add):


$$
\frac{1}{\tau_n^2} = \frac{1}{\tau_0^2} + \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2}{\sigma^2}
$$



**Posterior mean** (precision-weighted):


$$
\mu_n = \tau_n^2\left(\frac{\mu_0}{\tau_0^2} + \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i}{\sigma^2}\right)
$$



The posterior precision update shows:

> Posterior precision = prior precision + data precision.

The posterior becomes more certain when the prior is strong (small $\tau_0^2$), observation noise is small (small $\sigma^2$), or the inputs contain more information (large $\sum x_i^2$).

<Callout type="tip" title="Connection to ordinary least squares" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

In this no-intercept model, the OLS estimate is $\hat{\beta}_{\text{OLS}} = \frac{\sum x_i y_i}{\sum x_i^2}$.

As the prior variance $\tau_0^2 \to \infty$, prior precision $\to 0$, and the posterior mean approaches $\hat{\beta}_{\text{OLS}}$. OLS is the **weak-prior limit** of Bayesian linear regression.

</Callout>

---

## 5. Making Predictions

For a new input $x_*$, the model predicts:


$$
y_* = \beta x_* + \epsilon_*, \qquad \epsilon_* \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)
$$



In Bayesian regression, we don't plug in a single $\beta$. We average over all plausible values using the posterior — yielding the **posterior predictive distribution**.

### Predictive Mean


$$
\mathbb{E}[y_* \mid x_*, D] = x_*\,\mu_n
$$



### Predictive Variance

The predictive variance has two additive sources:

1. **Coefficient uncertainty:** $\text{Var}(\beta x_* \mid D) = x_*^2 \tau_n^2$
2. **Observation noise:** $\text{Var}(\epsilon_*) = \sigma^2$


$$
\text{Var}(y_* \mid x_*, D) = x_*^2 \tau_n^2 + \sigma^2
$$



The full posterior predictive distribution is:


$$
\boxed{y_* \mid x_*,D \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(x_*\,\mu_n,\;\; x_*^2\tau_n^2 + \sigma^2\right)}
$$



---

## 6. Credible Intervals for Predictions

A 95% credible interval for a future observation $y_*$:


$$
x_*\,\mu_n \;\pm\; 1.96\sqrt{x_*^2\tau_n^2 + \sigma^2}
$$



<Callout type="note" title="Credible interval for the mean response only" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

If we only want uncertainty about the **mean line** itself (what is the likely mean response at $x_*$, not the spread of individual observations), we exclude $\sigma^2$:


$$
x_*\,\mu_n \;\pm\; 1.96\,|x_*|\,\tau_n
$$



</Callout>

### Why the Intervals Grow with $x_*$

The prediction variance scales as $x_*^2 \tau_n^2$, so the standard deviation grows **linearly with $|x_*|$**. This creates a "bowtie" or "megaphone" shape around the regression line.

Intuitively: we are only estimating a slope $\beta$ and forcing the line through the origin. Imagine a long stick pinned at $(0, 0)$. We're slightly uncertain about the exact angle. Right at the origin the stick doesn't move at all. But the further out you look, that same tiny angular wobble translates into a large vertical swing.

---

## 7. Worked Example

*Assumes known $\sigma = 1.0$, prior $\beta \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, 2.0^2)$.*

We observe three data points: $x = [1.0, 2.0, 3.0]$, $y = [1.2, 2.5, 2.7]$.

**Summary statistics:**


$$
\sum x_i^2 = 1.0 + 4.0 + 9.0 = 14.0, \qquad \sum x_i y_i = 1.2 + 5.0 + 8.1 = 14.3
$$



**Posterior precision:**


$$
\frac{1}{\tau_n^2} = \frac{1}{2.0^2} + \frac{14.0}{1.0^2} = 0.25 + 14.0 = 14.25 \implies \tau_n^2 \approx 0.070
$$



**Posterior mean:**


$$
\mu_n = 0.070 \times \left(\frac{0}{2.0^2} + \frac{14.3}{1.0^2}\right) = 0.070 \times 14.3 \approx 1.00
$$



**Posterior:** $\beta \mid D \sim \mathcal{N}(1.00,\; 0.070)$

The figure below shows the posterior mean line (slope $\approx 1.00$) with:
- **Inner shaded region:** 95% credible interval for the mean response $\beta x_*$ (slope uncertainty only)
- **Outer shaded region:** 95% predictive interval for future observations (includes $\sigma^2$)
