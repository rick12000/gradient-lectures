Bayes' rule provides a mathematical framework for updating our beliefs about a hypothesis when we observe new evidence.

## Derivation from First Principles

The conditional probability formula states that the probability of event $A$ occurring given that event $B$ has occurred is the probability of both occurring divided by the probability of $B$:


$$
P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}
$$



By symmetry, the probability of $B$ given $A$ is:


$$
P(B|A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}
$$



We can isolate the joint probability $P(A \cap B)$ in both equations:


$$
P(A \cap B) = P(A|B)P(B)
$$



$$
P(A \cap B) = P(B|A)P(A)
$$



Since both expressions equal $P(A \cap B)$, we can set them equal to each other:


$$
P(A|B)P(B) = P(B|A)P(A)
$$



Dividing both sides by $P(B)$ gives us Bayes' rule:

<div class="math-result">

$$
P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)}
$$

</div>

This formula tells us how to *invert* conditional probabilities. If we know the probability of the evidence given the hypothesis $P(B|A)$, we can find the probability of the hypothesis given the evidence $P(A|B)$.

## The Law of Total Probability

The denominator $P(B)$ in Bayes' rule represents the absolute probability of observing the evidence. In practice, this is often problematic because we rarely know $P(B)$ directly. We usually only know how likely the evidence is *under specific conditions*.

To make the denominator computable, we use the **Law of Total Probability**. If an event $A$ and its complement $A'$ (not $A$) partition the entire sample space, then event $B$ can only occur in two ways: either alongside $A$, or alongside $A'$.

The total probability of $B$ is the sum of these two mutually exclusive possibilities:


$$
P(B) = P(B \cap A) + P(B \cap A')
$$



Using the conditional probability formula, we can expand these joint probabilities:


$$
P(B) = P(B|A)P(A) + P(B|A')P(A')
$$



Substituting this expansion back into the denominator of Bayes' rule gives us the final, fully computable formulation:

<div class="math-step">

$$
P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B|A)P(A) + P(B|A')P(A')}
$$

</div>

<Callout type="example" title="Worked Example: Rare Disease Testing" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

Suppose a new test for a rare disease is developed. We know the following:

- **Prevalence:** 1% of the population has the disease, $P(D) = 0.01$.
- **Sensitivity:** If you have the disease, the test is positive 99% of the time, $P(+|D) = 0.99$.
- **False Positive Rate:** If you do not have the disease, the test is positive 5% of the time, $P(+|D') = 0.05$.

You take the test and get a positive result. What is the **posterior** probability you actually have the disease, $P(D|+)$?

Without Bayes' rule, one might intuitively guess the probability is around 95% or 99%. Let's calculate the exact posterior probability.

First, calculate $P(D') = 1 - P(D) = 0.99$.

Then, compute the total probability of a positive test:


$$
P(+) = P(+|D)P(D) + P(+|D')P(D')
$$



$$
P(+) = (0.99 \times 0.01) + (0.05 \times 0.99) = 0.0099 + 0.0495 = 0.0594
$$



Now apply Bayes' rule:


$$
P(D|+) = \frac{P(+|D)P(D)}{P(+)} = \frac{0.0099}{0.0594} \approx 0.1667
$$



Even with a positive result on a highly sensitive test, the probability of actually having the disease is only about **16.7%**. The disease is rare, meaning most people won't have it. While the test accurately catches most people who already have the disease, applying it to the general population yields a high absolute number of false positives. These false positives overwhelm the true positives, driving down the final probability.

</Callout>
