Ever tested positive for a rare disease and immediately assumed the worst?

Bayes' rule provides a mathematical framework for updating our beliefs about a hypothesis when we observe new evidence. It explains why a positive result on a highly accurate test might still mean you are probably healthy.

## Derivation from First Principles

Bayes' rule is derived directly from the definition of conditional probability.

<Callout type="info" title="Derivation: Bayes' Rule" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

The conditional probability formula states that the probability of event $A$ occurring given that event $B$ has occurred is the probability of both occurring divided by the probability of $B$:

$$
P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}
$$

By symmetry, the probability of $B$ given $A$ is:

$$
P(B|A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}
$$

We can isolate the joint probability $P(A \cap B)$ in both equations:

$$
P(A \cap B) = P(A|B)P(B)
$$

$$
P(A \cap B) = P(B|A)P(A)
$$

Since both expressions equal $P(A \cap B)$, we can set them equal to each other:

$$
P(A|B)P(B) = P(B|A)P(A)
$$

Dividing both sides by $P(B)$ gives us Bayes' rule.

</Callout>

The resulting theorem is **Bayes' Rule**:

$$
P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)}
$$

This formula tells us how to invert conditional probabilities. If we know the probability of the evidence given the hypothesis $P(B|A)$, we can find the probability of the hypothesis given the evidence $P(A|B)$.

## The Law of Total Probability

The denominator $P(B)$ in Bayes' rule represents the absolute probability of observing the evidence. In practice, this is often problematic because we rarely know $P(B)$ directly. We usually only know how likely the evidence is *under specific conditions* (e.g., how likely a positive test is if you have a disease, versus if you don't).

To make the denominator computable, we use the **Law of Total Probability**. If an event $A$ and its complement $A'$ (not $A$) partition the entire sample space, then event $B$ can only occur in two ways: either alongside $A$, or alongside $A'$.

The total probability of $B$ is the sum of these two mutually exclusive possibilities:

$$
P(B) = P(B \cap A) + P(B \cap A')
$$

Using the conditional probability formula, we can expand these joint probabilities:

$$
P(B) = P(B|A)P(A) + P(B|A')P(A')
$$

Substituting this expansion back into the denominator of Bayes' rule gives us the final, fully computable formulation:

$$
P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B|A)P(A) + P(B|A')P(A')}
$$

<Callout type="example" title="Worked Example: Rare Disease Testing" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

Suppose a new test for a rare disease is developed. We know the following:
- **Prevalence:** 1% of the population has the disease, or $P(D) = 0.01$.
- **Sensitivity:** If you have the disease, the test is positive 99% of the time, or $P(+|D) = 0.99$.
- **False Positive Rate:** If you do not have the disease, the test is positive 5% of the time, or $P(+|D') = 0.05$.

You take the test and get a positive result. What is the **posterior** probability you actually have the disease, $P(D|+)$?

Without Bayes' rule, one might intuitively guess the probability is around 95% or 99%. Let's calculate the exact posterior probability.

First, calculate the total probability of a positive test, $P(+)$. We need the probability of not having the disease, which is $P(D') = 1 - P(D) = 0.99$.

$$
P(+) = P(+|D)P(D) + P(+|D')P(D')
$$

$$
P(+) = (0.99 \times 0.01) + (0.05 \times 0.99)
$$

$$
P(+) = 0.0099 + 0.0495 = 0.0594
$$

Now, apply Bayes' rule:

$$
P(D|+) = \frac{P(+|D)P(D)}{P(+)}
$$

$$
P(D|+) = \frac{0.0099}{0.0594} \approx 0.1667
$$

Even with a positive result on a highly sensitive test, the probability of actually having the disease is only about **16.7%**.

</Callout>

<Callout type="note" title="The Base Rate Fallacy">

The reason for the counterintuitive result in the example above is that the disease is rare, meaning most people won't have it. While the test accurately catches most people who already have the disease, applying it to the general population yields a high absolute number of false positives. These false positives overwhelm the true positives, driving down the final probability. Ignoring the underlying prevalence is known as the base rate fallacy.

</Callout>
