When controlling for confounders (identified via your [DAG](/tracks/causal-inference/dag/)), the core intuition behind Double Machine Learning (DML) is simple: isolate the variation in the treatment $T$ and the outcome $Y$ that is *not* explained by the confounders $X$.

By analyzing the relationship between the *residual* treatment and the *residual* outcome, we extract the true causal effect of $T$ on $Y$, completely independent of $X$.

## The Partially Linear Model

We formalize this using the Partially Linear Model. We assume the outcome $Y$ is driven by the treatment $T$ plus the impact of the confounders $X$:

$$
Y = \theta T + g(X) + \epsilon
$$

- **$\theta$**: The causal effect we want to estimate.
- **$g(X)$**: A "nuisance function" capturing the complex relationship mapping the confounders $X$ to the outcome $Y$.
- **$\epsilon$**: Random noise.

Furthermore, treatment assignment itself is affected by $X$. We define a second equation for $T$:

$$
T = m(X) + \eta
$$

- **$m(X)$**: A second nuisance function mapping confounders to treatment assignment. Estimating $m(X)$ is essentially a [propensity modeling](/tracks/causal-inference/propensity-scores/) exercise.
- **$\eta$**: Random noise in treatment assignment.

## Orthogonalization and Residualization

To estimate $\theta$, DML uses machine learning to estimate the nuisance functions $g(X)$ and $m(X)$, and partials them out.

**The Procedure:**
1. **Model the Outcome:** Train an ML model to predict $Y$ from $X$. Compute the outcome residuals: $\tilde{Y} = Y - \hat{Y}$.
2. **Model the Treatment:** Train an ML model to predict $T$ from $X$. Compute the treatment residuals: $\tilde{T} = T - \hat{T}$.
3. **Regress:** Run an Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression of $\tilde{Y}$ on $\tilde{T}$.

<Callout type="note" title="Interpreting the Coefficient">

The coefficient $\hat{\theta}$ from this final regression is your Average Treatment Effect (ATE). Because we regressed the unexplained variance in $Y$ on the unexplained variance in $T$, $\hat{\theta}$ represents the expected change in $Y$ caused by a one-unit change in $T$, completely stripped of any confounding influence from $X$.

</Callout>

<Callout type="info" title="Derivation: Why Residualization Works" collapsible defaultOpen={false}>

Taking the expectation of the outcome conditional on $X$:

$$
\mathbb{E}[Y|X] = \theta \mathbb{E}[T|X] + g(X)
$$

Subtracting this from the original outcome equation:

$$
Y - \mathbb{E}[Y|X] = (\theta T + g(X) + \epsilon) - (\theta \mathbb{E}[T|X] + g(X))
$$

Let $\tilde{Y} = Y - \mathbb{E}[Y|X]$ and $\tilde{T} = T - \mathbb{E}[T|X]$. The complex nuisance function $g(X)$ perfectly cancels out:

$$
\tilde{Y} = \theta \tilde{T} + \epsilon
$$

This leaves a clean linear relationship between the residuals, allowing us to estimate $\theta$ directly via OLS.

</Callout>

## Practical Training: Cross-Fitting

There is a practical catch when estimating the residuals.

<Callout type="warning" title="The Overfitting Problem">

If you train an ML model to predict $Y$ from $X$ and then calculate the residuals $\tilde{Y}$ on that *same* training data, the model will overfit. It memorizes the noise, making the residuals artificially small and biasing the final causal estimate $\hat{\theta}$. To get "honest" residuals, predictions must be made out-of-sample.

</Callout>

To solve this, DML requires **cross-fitting**. We must ensure that the residuals for any given observation are generated by a model that never saw that observation during training.

**The Cross-Fitting Procedure:**
1. **Split:** Partition the dataset into $K$ folds.
2. **Train & Predict:** For each fold $k$:
   - Train the outcome model and treatment model using all data *except* fold $k$.
   - Predict $\hat{Y}$ and $\hat{T}$ for the observations inside fold $k$.
3. **Residualize:** Compute the out-of-fold residuals for all observations:

$$
\tilde{Y} = Y - \hat{Y}
$$

$$
\tilde{T} = T - \hat{T}
$$

4. **Estimate:** Regress $\tilde{Y}$ on $\tilde{T}$ to extract the causal effect $\hat{\theta}$.
